# Appendix A — Glossary

A quick reference to the vocabulary used in this book and in MECE Studio.

**Action title (so-what).** The one-sentence conclusion a split's branches add up
to, stated as a finding rather than a topic ("Volume fell 5% on Q3 losses", not
"Volume analysis") — Barbara Minto's term. In MECE Studio you set it on a split's
Logic tab; the synthesis leads the branch with it, and the root's action title is
the governing thought.

**Answer-first.** A way of communicating that leads with the conclusion, then
supports it — rather than walking the audience through your analysis and revealing
the answer at the end. Also called *top-down* or the *pyramid principle*.

**Branch.** A node in an issue tree together with everything beneath it — a
sub-question and its own sub-issues.

**Coaching advisory.** A light, `info`-level nudge on the *quality* of a tree's
thinking — a bare one-word branch, an over-long "laundry list" split, an altitude
outlier, a hypothesis still phrased as a question — shown in MECE Studio's Coaching
callout. Deliberately separate from the ME / CE checks: coaching never counts
toward the MECE health chip.

**Collectively exhaustive (CE).** A set of parts that, taken together, cover the
whole — nothing is left out. The "CE" in MECE.

**Deductive split.** A split whose children are the steps of an argument chain
(premise → premise → conclusion) rather than parts of a whole. MECE does not apply
— the steps are meant to build on each other — so the check is whether the chain
holds. Contrast *inductive split*.

**Dimension.** The single axis a split cuts on — by geography, by customer type,
by stage. One consistent dimension per level is the backbone of a mutually
exclusive split; in MECE Studio you can name it on a split's Logic tab.

**Driver.** A quantitative input that moves a number you care about. In a
value-driver tree, the leaves are drivers (price, volume, cost per unit) and the
root is the outcome (profit, revenue).

**Evidence.** A fact, datapoint, or observation that supports or contradicts a
hypothesis. In MECE Studio, evidence is attached to a node with a direction
(supporting / contradicting) and a strength.

**Exhaustiveness.** See *collectively exhaustive*.

**Governing answer.** The one-sentence answer the whole tree argues for — stated
on day one, tested by the branches, and delivered first in an answer-first
synthesis. In MECE Studio it lives in the Answer banner above the canvas.

**How tree.** A prescriptive tree that lays out and compares ways to act ("How do
we restore margin?"). A tree should ask one direction — why or how — not both.
Contrast *why tree*.

**Hypothesis.** A candidate answer you intend to test. Hypothesis-driven problem
solving starts from a best guess and uses the tree to confirm or kill it, rather
than analysing everything in the hope an answer emerges.

**Inductive split.** A split that cuts a whole into parts of the same kind
(regions, cost lines, stages), which MECE tests for overlaps and gaps. The default
kind of split. Contrast *deductive split*.

**Issue tree.** A hierarchical breakdown of a question into sub-questions and
sub-issues, so that answering the small questions answers the big one. The central
artifact of this book.

**Key question.** The single, sharp question the whole tree exists to answer. A
good key question is specific, decision-relevant, and answerable.

**MECE.** *Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive.* A property of a *split*:
the parts don't overlap (ME) and together cover everything (CE). A MECE split is
clean to reason about and safe to divide work across.

**Mutually exclusive (ME).** A set of parts that don't overlap — each item belongs
to exactly one part. The "ME" in MECE.

**Node.** A single box in the tree: a question, sub-issue, or driver.

**Ordering principle.** The order a split's branches are read in — by *importance*
(priority), by *time* (a sequence), or by *structure* (a fixed partition). Barbara
Minto's three orders. In MECE Studio a split's ordering principle overrides the
global "sort siblings by priority" setting for that split.

**Prioritisation.** Deciding which branches to work first. In this book,
prioritisation is impact × ease: chase the branches that matter and are cheap to
test.

**Problem brief.** A one-page framing of the problem behind the key question —
situation, complication, owner, decision-makers, success criteria, scope, and
desired outcome (the Problem Identity Card). In MECE Studio, opened from the Answer
banner.

**Root.** The top node of the tree — the key question.

**Scaffold.** A set of starter sub-issues that a decomposition type suggests
(binary → A / not-A, segments → parts + Other). A scaffold is a prompt, not an
answer; you rename it.

**Sensitivity.** How much an outcome moves when one driver changes. A sensitivity
analysis ranks the drivers by their effect, so you know which number to fight over.

**Split.** A parent node together with its immediate children — the unit MECE is
judged on. A tree is a stack of splits.

**Sub-issue.** A child question in the tree; a piece of the question above it.

**Synthesis.** Reading the tree back as an answer — pulling the findings up the
branches into a single, prioritised story.

**Value-driver tree.** An issue tree whose splits are arithmetic (sum, product,
difference), so numbers on the leaves roll up into a number at the root.

**Why tree.** A diagnostic tree that breaks a problem into its possible causes
("Why have profits fallen?"). A tree should ask one direction — why or how — not
both. Contrast *how tree*.
